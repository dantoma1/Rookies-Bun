It is one man — Mick Timmermans, the External Affairs Officer — who has already, in writing, declared himself a fan of the platform you are positioning against. ## I. The Macro Context — Why Now Is Actually a Good Moment

You are not pitching into a vacuum. The reception you get on May 7 will be shaped, whether Mick articulates it or not, by three structural forces in the European early-career landscape that have shifted significantly in the last 24 months.

**The Dutch labor market for early-career talent has tightened, then bifurcated.** Through 2024 and into 2025, hiring volumes for graduates and interns at large Dutch firms compressed — fewer slots, more applicants per slot. Boutique firms and mid-size SMEs, which always had hiring needs, became the relative growth segment but lacked the recruiting infrastructure to compete with the visibility of EY, PwC, ASML, and the rest. The result is a paradox students experience daily: it is harder than ever to find a role at a top-twenty employer, and surprisingly easy to be invisible to firms that would gladly hire you. Mick almost certainly recognizes this paradox in his own corporate-relations work — it is the texture of the conversations he has with smaller employers who tell him *"we'd love TiU students; we just don't know how to reach them."* This is your wedge, even if he doesn't yet articulate it that way.

**TiU is operating under intensifying employment-outcome scrutiny.** Dutch universities are increasingly judged — formally for accreditation, informally in international rankings — on graduate employment outcomes. The Vereniging van Universiteiten (VSNU) has accelerated this trend; international rankings (THE, QS) weight employer reputation and graduate-employment metrics more than they did five years ago. Tilburg specifically competes for international student tuition with universities offering more obvious career outcomes (Erasmus, IE, Bocconi). For TiU's management, employability data is becoming a strategic asset — not a bureaucratic checkbox. This is invisible context for your skills-gap dashboard pitch: it lands not as "here is a nice tool" but as "here is data you are about to be asked for whether you have it or not."

**The post-COVID employer-recruitment behavior has fundamentally shifted toward digital-first, hybrid models.** Pre-2020, big-company on-campus recruiting was a heavy-presence, high-touch ritual; smaller firms simply opted out because the logistics were prohibitive. Post-2020, on-campus is a supplement, not the primary channel. This levels the playing field in a way that disproportionately benefits SMEs *if* they have a digital channel that works for them. JobTeaser was designed for the pre-2020 world (premium-employer-pays gates designed for big-company recruiters); Rookies is designed for the post-2020 world (free unlimited posting, algorithmic discovery, low-friction outreach). You are not pitching against an inferior product; you are pitching against a product whose design assumptions have aged.

When Mick objects to Rookies on the grounds that JobTeaser works, the unstated assumption is that what worked in 2018 still works in 2026. That assumption is contestable, and contesting it is part of the conversation — but never directly. Never tell Mick his current setup is outdated. Show him, through your demo and your dashboard, what the post-2020 version looks like and let him recognize it himself.

## II. The Competitive Landscape — Reading JobTeaser Honestly

To convert Mick, you need to understand JobTeaser more rigorously than he does. Most career-services managers know their platform operationally — the back-office contact, the posting flow, the events module — but they rarely think about it structurally. You should.

JobTeaser is a French company founded in 2008. It is now in 800+ universities across 27 countries and has roughly 5 million students and 250,000 recruiters in its system. Their commercial model is straightforward and revealing: universities pay nothing; corporate recruiters pay through a freemium ladder (free tier limited to 4 postings per year per institution, premium gated for firms with 5+ hires per year). Universities can purchase optional modules — including "matchmaking" — but most do not, and TiU specifically does not. Their core product is a bulletin board with strong operational support and a few ancillary tools (events, orientation tools called Marco and Freya, ATS integrations via Kombo).

This shape is not accidental. It is a direct consequence of the commercial incentive structure. When universities don't pay, they cannot demand product evolution; their power is limited. When companies pay, the product evolves toward what companies want — branded employer pages, multi-school posting, ATS integration. Student-side experience is a cost center: it has to be good enough that universities don't churn (which is rare anyway), but no better than that. The result is a platform optimized for *posting volume* and *recruiter convenience*, not for *student engagement* or *strategic university outcomes*.

This is why Mick's email reads the way it does. He praises JobTeaser for being free, for having operational support, for having modules he can buy if needed. He does not praise it for student engagement, matching quality, or strategic data. Those things are not what JobTeaser was built to deliver, so they are not what he uses to evaluate it.

**The gaps you attack are not bugs in JobTeaser. They are features of its commercial design.** Your competitive position is therefore not "we are better at the same thing" — it is "we are organized differently because our incentive structure is different." JobTeaser's matching is gated behind premium because matching is expensive to operate at scale; their analytics are engagement-focused because engagement metrics serve their paying customers (recruiters) more than strategic data would; SMEs are locked out because the freemium ladder is designed to upsell them, not serve them.

Rookies' equivalent positioning, when articulated correctly, lands as: *"We made different design choices because we are trying to solve a different problem. The result is a tool that does specific things JobTeaser structurally cannot do well, while leaving alone the things JobTeaser does perfectly fine."* This framing matters because it sidesteps the trap of "incumbent vs. challenger" — a frame in which the incumbent always wins because they have momentum — and replaces it with "complementary tools for different problems," a frame Mick can adopt without his having to admit he was wrong about JobTeaser.

**A subtle observation about JobTeaser's market position that Mick almost certainly does not know:** TiU itself is not entirely on JobTeaser. TiSEM — the Tilburg School of Economics & Management, where you and Cristian sit — runs a separate platform at careerplatformtilburg.nl, distinct from the central TiU JobTeaser deployment. This means TiU has *already implicitly accepted* that different schools can have different career platforms. The "switching cost is too high" objection that Mick will raise is structurally weaker than he realizes, because TiSEM has already made the move he's defending against. If the conversation goes well enough to make this point, you do not have to make it as a confrontation; you can mention it neutrally as context: *"I noticed TiSEM runs its own platform separately — that's actually one of the reasons I think a parallel pilot here would feel familiar rather than disruptive."*

Use this insight only if the moment is right. Bringing it up too early reads as point-scoring. Bringing it up at the right moment reads as strategic intelligence.

## III. The Student Reality — What Mick Doesn't See From His Office

Mick spends his days managing corporate relationships, posting workflows, and the operational work of running a career-services platform. He does not, on a daily basis, sit with TiU students and watch how they search for internships and graduate roles. The actual student experience — which you have lived recently and which is the source of your founding insight — is therefore a layer Mick does not have direct access to. You do. This is an asymmetric information advantage you should use carefully.

Here is what is true about how TiU students actually search:

**Most never log into the JobTeaser-powered career portal.** They go to LinkedIn first because it is where their friends are. Magnet.me sometimes, especially for internships. Direct company career pages for the firms they already know. The TiU portal is not an active search destination for the median student; it is a place they visit once or twice in their final year when panic sets in. This is not a JobTeaser failure; it is a category-wide failure of every university-portal product. Career platforms compete with consumer social networks for attention they will lose.

**The search becomes urgent only late, and badly.** Most TiU students enter their final year of master's or bachelor's with a vague intention to "start applying" and a real start date roughly 3 months before graduation. Two-thirds of the class compresses six months of search into ten weeks. The platform that is best at supporting that compressed-panic phase wins the engagement, and JobTeaser's bulletin-board model is poorly suited to it (no personalization, no surfaced opportunities, no algorithmic urgency).

**International students experience the search as significantly worse.** TiU is roughly 30% international. Most career platforms — JobTeaser included — are linguistically and culturally tuned to native Dutch students. Postings default to Dutch. Filtering by visa-friendly employers is not a default feature. International students therefore do not engage with the portal at the same rate as Dutch students, and their employment outcomes lag accordingly. This is a TiU strategic risk (it affects international student satisfaction, retention, and rankings) and an unaddressed market opportunity. Rookies' explicit international support is a thin advantage today (the features are built but not heavily used) but a real positioning lever.

**Career services' "1-on-1 personal guidance" model — which Mick prizes — works for the students who already engage.** It does not reach the students who don't. The students who book 1-on-1 appointments are disproportionately the ones who are already doing their search well; the students who would benefit most from intervention are the ones who never walk in. This is not Mick's fault — it is structural — but it does mean that his philosophical defense of "guidance over platforms" has a blind spot. The platform is not a substitute for guidance; it is a discovery surface for the students who would otherwise never trigger guidance.

You should not say any of this to Mick directly. He will hear it as criticism of his work. Instead, frame it as something Rookies wants to *learn from* him: *"You probably know better than anyone which kinds of students engage with the current platform and which don't — that's the thing we want to design around. Where do you see the gaps?"* He will tell you the truth in his answer. You do not have to put words in his mouth.

## IV. Mick Timmermans — A Deep Read

You have one document from which to triangulate Mick's psychology: his email reply to Herbert. It is short, but a careful reading yields more than its length suggests. Read it three times before May 7.

What it tells you, in order of importance:

**Mick is operationally focused, not strategically focused.** His evaluation criteria for JobTeaser are: free, back-office support, useful modules. He does not mention student outcomes, employer satisfaction, accreditation data, or strategic positioning. This is not because he is shallow; it is because his job is operational. His mandate is keeping the corporate-partnership pipeline running smoothly. That is what he is measured on, formally and informally, and that is the lens through which he evaluates everything. *Implication: every Rookies advantage you describe must, somewhere in your sentence, connect to operational reality. Strategic value alone will not move him.*

**Mick has rehearsed defending JobTeaser before.** The phrasing in his email — *"the platform is free, and we can count on a strong back office that supports us daily"* — has the cadence of someone who has said it many times. He has been challenged before, by colleagues or by other vendors, and he has a stock answer. This is not a sign of intellectual rigidity; it is a sign that he has been pitched many times by people with worse products than yours. Many of his defenses are not deep convictions; they are dismissive scripts that work on most pitches. *Implication: do not engage his defenses head-on. Each direct engagement triggers another rehearsed line. Bypass them by changing the frame.*

**Mick philosophically prizes human connection over platform technology.** His statement that career services "strongly focus on personal, offline guidance through 1-on-1 conversations" is not just a fact; it is a value claim. He believes that the human work is the real work and the platform is logistics. This is partly true and partly self-serving (it elevates the importance of his team), but it is genuinely held. *Implication: any pitch that positions Rookies as a "platform that solves the problem" will trigger his defense. Reframe Rookies as "the data layer that makes the human work sharper" — supporting his work, not replacing it.*

**Mick is tired of vendor pitches.** This is not in the email, but it is true of every external-affairs officer at every major university. He gets pitched constantly. Most of the pitches sound the same. He is implicitly optimizing for "how do I get this person out of my office without committing to anything," which is the safe default for a busy operational manager. *Implication: the worst version of your meeting is one that pattern-matches to "another vendor pitch." Your job is to be the meeting that did not pattern-match — the one where someone listened first, treated him as the expert, and asked his help shaping something rather than asking him to buy something.*

**Mick has personal incentives that are quieter than his stated ones.** He is paid to maintain the corporate-partnership pipeline at TiU. He is also, as a human, motivated by being seen as innovative, by being credited for bringing in initiatives that work, and by avoiding being blamed for ones that fail. The math of "should I let this in" therefore has a hidden term: *"if I bring this to Herbert and it works, I get credit; if I bring it and it fails, I get blame; the safest path is to not bring it at all."* Your job is to reduce his perceived blame-risk while increasing his perceived credit-upside. The way to do this is to make the ask small (a parallel pilot, not a switch) and the upside concrete (a dashboard he can show Herbert immediately, even before the pilot launches).

**Mick does not have to like Rookies for the meeting to succeed.** This is the most important psychological note. You are not trying to win him over emotionally. You are trying to make him willing to take Rookies to Herbert as an exploration, not an endorsement. The lowest-risk verbal commitment from him at the end of the meeting is *"I think this is interesting enough that we should bring it up with Herbert — without me committing to advocate for it yet."* That is a win. Do not push for more. Pushing for more triggers his rehearsed defenses.

## V. The Conversion Arc — How To Move A Skeptic Without Selling

There is a body of professional knowledge — sales psychology, negotiation theory, motivational interviewing — that addresses exactly the situation you face on May 7. The summary is this: skeptics are not converted by better arguments. They are converted by being heard, by being given agency, and by being shown a version of the future in which they are the protagonist rather than the convert.

The technique that maps best to your situation is called **enrollment**, distinct from selling. In selling, you persuade someone to accept your offer. In enrollment, you invite someone to co-design something they will then advocate for as if it were their own. Selling produces buyers; enrollment produces champions. You are trying to produce a champion.

The mechanics of enrollment, applied to Mick:

**Start by giving him the floor for longer than he expects.** When you walk into his office, his unconscious model of the meeting is that he will listen to you pitch for 20 minutes, ask three skeptical questions, and end the meeting having maintained his position. Your opening line should disrupt that model. *"I'd actually rather understand your situation first. You've been running TiU's external-affairs function for years; you've worked with JobTeaser long enough to know exactly where it works and where it doesn't. I'd love to hear that perspective before I tell you anything about Rookies."* And then — and this is the hard part — be quiet. Let him talk. Take notes. Visible note-taking signals that what he is saying is important.

**Listen for what he is not saying.** He will tell you, in roundabout ways, what frustrates him about his current setup. He may not phrase it as frustration. He may say "JobTeaser handles this fine, although sometimes…" or "we've thought about doing X but…" — these qualifiers are the openings. They are the places where his defended position has weak points. Note them carefully.

**Reflect his words back, accurately, before responding.** When you eventually do respond, your responses should begin with phrases like *"You mentioned X — that's actually exactly the kind of problem we've been trying to solve"* or *"What you said about Y is interesting because it lines up with something we built specifically for that case."* Citing him back to himself does several things: it proves you listened, it makes him feel respected, and it positions every Rookies advantage as an answer to a problem *he* identified, not a problem *you* told him he had.

**Lower the temperature on disagreement.** When Mick says something defensive about JobTeaser, do not contradict him. Agree, partially. *"You're right about that — and the question I've been wrestling with is whether there's a complementary layer that adds something specifically to TiU's strategic position that JobTeaser wasn't designed to deliver."* This phrasing keeps the conversation collaborative even when you are introducing a critique. It treats him as an intellectual peer, not as someone who needs correcting.

**Position the demo as exploration, not proof.** When you eventually show him Rookies, the framing matters more than the demo itself. *"Let me show you a few specific things and you tell me which would actually help and which wouldn't"* invites him to evaluate. *"Let me show you what we built"* invites him to judge. These sound similar but produce different responses. Use the first.

**Give him the pen.** Late in the meeting, ask: *"If we were going to design this for TiU specifically — what should it look like? What's missing from what I've shown you? What would actually move the needle for TiU's management?"* This is the heart of enrollment. By asking him to design the thing, you make the resulting design partially his. He is now building it with you, not buying it from you. The next conversation he has — internally, with himself — is no longer "should I let this vendor in?" but "how do I make this work?"

**Make the ask small enough to be safe.** The only commitment he should be asked to make at the end of the meeting is whether he is willing to discuss this further with Herbert. Not whether he endorses it. Not whether he will champion it. Just whether he thinks it is worth Herbert hearing about. This is a low-risk yes for him, and it is the only yes you actually need.

## VI. Soft Skills — The Mechanics That Compound

The substance of what you say will not be the only signal Mick reads. He will also read tone, body language, pacing, attire, and a hundred other micro-cues that shape his unconscious assessment of whether you are someone he wants to spend institutional capital on.

**Tone.** Calm and warm, not energetic. Founders pitching to operational managers often default to high-energy enthusiasm; this reads as "trying to sell me something." Lower your energy by 20% from your natural state. Speak slowly. Use silence comfortably. The most experienced consultants pause for 2-3 seconds before answering important questions; it signals deliberation, not hesitation, and it makes your eventual answer feel more considered.

**Body language.** Sit slightly forward, hands visible, eye contact direct but not intense. Mirror his posture subtly — if he leans back, you eventually lean back. If he leans in, you lean in. This is not manipulation; it is an unconscious signal that you are tracking his state. Avoid gestures that read as nervous (hand-fidgeting, leg-jiggling, pen-clicking). Nod while listening, not in the eager way of someone seeking approval, but in the slow, deliberate way of someone weighing what is being said.

**Pacing.** Most pitches go faster than the founder thinks they do. Slow down by another 20%. The biggest mistake nervous founders make is to fill silences with more product detail. Don't. When Mick pauses to think, let him. When you finish a point, stop. The next sentence is his, not yours. Comfort with silence is the single most underrated skill in conversations like this.

**Attire.** Dutch academic-business smart. A sharp-but-not-shiny shirt, no tie, dress shoes or clean leather sneakers, dark trousers. No suit (reads as pitching). No casualwear (reads as not taking it seriously). Mick will be in similar attire; match within ±10%.

**Language.** Use his vocabulary. He talks about "Career Services," "the AD's office," "students," "employers," "the platform." Use those words. Avoid startup vocabulary — "scale," "MVP," "go-to-market," "user acquisition." That language signals you live in a different world from him. He needs to feel that you live in his world, not that you've parachuted in from one. The exception: if he uses tech vocabulary (he might), match him there too.

**Names.** Use his name twice in the meeting — once early, once near the end. Three times is too many; once is too few. Names create a sense of personal connection at a level just below conscious processing. The early use should be in your opening; the late use should be in your closing ask.

**Cristian.** Acknowledge Cristian's role in setting this up, in the room, early. Something like *"Cristian, thank you for setting this up — I know it took some doing on your end."* This does three things: it credits Cristian publicly (he will be present, this matters to him), it signals to Mick that this meeting is faculty-backed (which raises his evaluation), and it positions you as someone who values relationships, not transactions.

**Notebook.** Bring a notebook and pen, paper, not digital. Take visible notes during his answers. This matters more than you think. When someone watches another person take notes on what they're saying, the speaker feels their words matter. This unlocks more candid speech, which gives you more material to work with later.

**The closing handshake.** Firm, brief, eye contact. *"Mick, thank you for the time. I'll send you a written follow-up tomorrow."* That sentence does three things: it ends the meeting on your terms (not his), it commits you publicly to a deliverable, and it gives you an excuse to email him in 24 hours without being awkward.

## VII. The Words That Win and the Words That Lose

A short list of phrases worth memorizing, because they have specific psychological effects.

**Phrases that open doors:**

- *"You'd know better than me — does this seem realistic?"* — Cedes expertise to Mick, makes him a collaborator.
- *"What would Career Services pushback be?"* — Surfaces objections proactively, makes him your scout.
- *"I'd want to learn from how you'd want to use this."* — Treats his use case as the design specification.
- *"That's a fair point. The way I've been thinking about it is…"* — Allows you to make a counterpoint without contradicting.
- *"Honest answer — I don't know yet."* — Builds enormous credibility when used sparingly. Founders who never say it lose trust over a 45-minute conversation.
- *"Cristian and I have been thinking about this together"* — Reminds him of the faculty backing without being heavy-handed about it.
- *"We're not asking for an answer today — just whether it's worth keeping the conversation going."* — Lowers the perceived ask to its actual size.

**Phrases that close doors:**

- *"You don't understand — JobTeaser doesn't actually do that."* — Triggers his rehearsed defense, makes the conversation adversarial.
- *"In my experience…"* — Reads as oversized confidence from a recent graduate.
- *"This is going to be a huge market."* — Pitch language. Activates his "another vendor" filter.
- *"We'll handle the implementation."* — Operational promise you can't credibly back. He will not believe it.
- *"Trust me on this."* — Trust is earned, not requested.
- *"I think you're going to love this."* — Tells him what to feel. Triggers reactance.
- Anything that frames the conversation as you-versus-JobTeaser. Frame it as TiU-versus-its-strategic-pressures, and Rookies as one tool that helps with one part of that.

## VIII. Reading Mick's Signals During the Meeting

You will learn more about how the meeting is going by watching Mick than by listening to your own performance. A few signals worth tracking:

**Positive signals:** He asks follow-up questions about specific features. He compares Rookies favorably to other vendors he's heard from (even neutrally — *"that's similar to X but actually better at…"*). He volunteers his own ideas for what Rookies should do. He mentions Herbert by name unprompted. He uses the word "we" or "us" when describing potential next steps. He asks about timeline or implementation, even hypothetically. He picks up on something specific in your demo and asks to see it again.

**Neutral signals:** He listens politely without engaging. He nods, but doesn't ask follow-up questions. He answers your questions correctly but briefly. He stays in evaluator mode — judging, not designing. This is not a loss; it means the meeting hasn't moved him yet. You still have time.

**Negative signals:** He repeatedly returns to the same defensive line ("JobTeaser is free…"). He redirects attention to operational concerns rather than strategic ones. He starts checking his phone or looking at the door. He brings the meeting back to its scheduled end-time precisely. He answers your questions with phrases like *"interesting, but…"* He references having to consult others before he could commit to anything (deflection). He says *"I'll think about it"* without proposing a next step.

If you see negative signals accumulating in the first 20 minutes, change tactics. Stop pitching. Ask more questions. Surface his real concerns. *"I'm getting the sense this isn't quite landing — what's the part that's not clicking for you?"* This is an aggressive move but it works, because it short-circuits the polite-rejection track. If he tells you the truth, you have something to work with. If he can't articulate what isn't working, his resistance is emotional, not analytical, and you can shift to a softer ask.

## IX. The Longer View — Why TiU Specifically Matters

Even with everything in this briefing executed well, your realistic odds for a TiU partnership end-state are roughly 5–15%. That is consistent with how university partnerships work in general; it is not a comment on Rookies' quality. Most university pitches die at one of the gates. The math is: *if you ran this same process at 8 Dutch universities with 5–15% success rates, you would land 1 or 2 partnerships. That is exactly what Handshake's early playbook looked like in the US.*

This implies two things about your strategic posture, both of which should inform how you walk into May 7:

**TiU is your best shot, not your only shot.** Erasmus, Maastricht, Eindhoven, Twente, Groningen, Leiden, UvA, Radboud — every one of these is a viable target. Several do not have JobTeaser-equivalent tools at their central level. Your professor's network and your alumni status give you warm-intro paths to several of them. If TiU says no, the loss is real but it is not terminal. The information you gain from a TiU "no" becomes ammunition for the next pitch.

**A single university partnership compounds.** This is the most important strategic insight to internalize. If TiU signs, the next conversation with Erasmus is not *"would you consider being our first partner?"* — it is *"here's what we're doing at TiU; we'd like to discuss bringing it to Erasmus."* The reference customer changes the conversation. One signed partnership is worth roughly five unsigned ones in terms of how it changes future negotiations. This is why TiU is worth fighting for even at low odds.

**The realistic 18-month outlook.** Plan for: 6–12 months from May 7 to a signed pilot at TiU (if it happens). 6 more months to a working pilot with real engagement metrics. Then renewal conversations and a second-university engagement starting. By month 18, the realistic best case is *Rookies has live deployments at 2–3 Dutch universities with combined ~3,000 active student profiles, a paying or semi-paying relationship with at least one of them, and a defensible story for institutional investors.* That is enough to credibly raise a pre-seed round of €300k–€600k. None of this is grand; all of it is real.

**The kill criterion, revisited.** Set yourself a date. If, by November 2026, Rookies does not have any university-level partnership signed (TiU or otherwise), reassess seriously. Don't grind into year three on a thesis that hasn't validated. The bar isn't "make money;" the bar is "have at least one institution willing to commit to using Rookies as a real tool, not as a curiosity." If you don't clear that bar in 6 months of dedicated effort across multiple universities, the thesis isn't working in this form, and you should consider whether to change the form or change the strategy.

## X. Final Counsel — What To Hold In Mind On May 7

A few things worth carrying into the meeting that don't fit neatly into the structured analysis above.

**Mick is not your enemy. He is a busy person doing his job.** The stance to bring is curiosity about him, not adversarial preparation against him. People can sense which one you walked in with within 30 seconds. The version of you that Mick will most respect is the one who treats him as a peer with a real perspective, even when his perspective is the obstacle to your goal. Founders who walk in radiating "I have to overcome this guy" usually fail. Founders who walk in radiating "I want to learn from this guy and find out if there's a real fit" usually succeed.

**Confidence and humility are not opposites.** The version of confidence that wins meetings like this is the kind that shows up as calm, prepared, and willing to admit uncertainty. The version that loses is the kind that shows up as bluster, certainty, and slick answers. You are 25, recently-graduated, building something with a younger brother who's still in school. You should not pretend to be older than you are. You should be unselfconscious about who you are while being unmistakably serious about the work.

**You are pitching the value, not pitching for approval.** A specific psychological reframe: most founders walk into meetings hoping the gatekeeper will validate them. This is a vulnerability the gatekeeper can sense. Mick's approval of you personally is not on the table; it shouldn't be your goal. What's on the table is whether Rookies is useful to TiU. Keep your sense of self-worth disconnected from his reaction. If he loves the platform, great. If he doesn't, fine — that's data about whether this particular fit works, not data about whether you're good enough. This emotional separation makes you significantly more effective in the room.

**Cristian is more important than Mick in the long run.** Whatever happens on May 7, your relationship with Cristian is a 5-year asset. He has already vouched for you. He will have other students, other contacts, other moments where his vouching matters. Treat that relationship as the asset it is — keep him updated, credit him publicly, send him a thank-you specifically separate from anything else you do. Even if Mick says no, Cristian will champion you to the next person. Your professor relationship will outlast this particular gatekeeper conversation.

**The meeting is research even when it's a sales call.** Whatever Mick says — whether it ends in a yes, a no, or a "maybe later" — you will learn things about how Dutch career-services managers evaluate platforms like Rookies. That learning is what makes your next pitch sharper. If he kills it, the meeting is not a loss; it is a transformed input into the Erasmus pitch, the Maastricht pitch, the Eindhoven pitch. Walk out with notes. Walk out with insights. Walk out with the names of his concerns articulated clearly so you can answer them better next time.

**One last thing.** The outcome of the meeting is not in your control. The performance of the meeting is.
