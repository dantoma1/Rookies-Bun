## Who Mick actually is

Read the email exchange one more time before the meeting. The framing matters enormously, and the framing your professor gave was wrong-by-omission.

- **Mick is TiU's External Affairs Officer.** Not the Career Services Head we initially assumed. His mandate is *external relationships* — corporate partnerships, employer engagement, TiU's reputation in the labor market.
- **He's a personal fan of JobTeaser.** He told Herbert this directly in writing. You're not selling against a neutral incumbent; you're selling against a tool he has actively defended.
- **He likes JobTeaser specifically because it makes his work easier.** Quote: *"the platform is free, and we can count on a strong back office that supports us daily."* His criteria are operational, not strategic.
- **He doesn't primarily care about student satisfaction.** That's Career Services' job. His job is keeping the corporate-partnership pipeline healthy.
- **He has a worldview about how this work happens.** Quote: *"From Career Services, we strongly focus on personal, offline guidance through 1-on-1 conversations."* Don't argue with this — he's earned that view through years on the ground.
- **He won't put effort in unless there's something in it for him.** This isn't cynicism — it's how every busy operational manager filters projects. You need to make this his project, not yours.

The meta-truth: Mick can't say yes alone, but he can absolutely say no alone. The management meeting won't happen if Mick walks out of May 7 unconvinced. So the goal isn't to sell him; the goal is to make sure he doesn't kill it.

## Things you do NOT volunteer in this meeting

Some details belong in your business plan, not in the dean's-office gatekeeper conversation. If Mick asks directly, deflect; do not lead with these.

- **How Rookies will charge companies long-term.** No mention of pricing tiers, premium employer subscriptions, monthly fees, or any company-side monetization. The pilot is free and that's the only commercial information that needs to be on the table on May 7.
- **The €5–10k post-September university subscription.** If asked, frame as: *"Too early — that's a conversation for after September, once we have data on whether the pilot delivered value."* Don't volunteer the number.
- **Investor conversations.** Even if true that you'll raise capital based on a TiU partnership, that's not Mick's problem and surfacing it reinforces the misperception that you want TiU's money. Stay quiet.
- **Competitor comparisons that flatter JobTeaser unnecessarily.** No "JobTeaser has 800 partner universities" — that's their market data, not yours, and citing it makes Rookies look small. Talk only about *what TiU specifically gets and where the structural gaps are*, not about JobTeaser's global stature.
- **Internal team size.** Don't volunteer "we're a 2-person team" or "I'm building this alongside finishing my master's." Say *"the founding team"* and *"we"* throughout. If pressed, be honest — but don't lead with it.
- **Your fundraising plans, equity structure, runway.** None of this is Mick's concern and all of it makes the pitch sound startup-y rather than partnership-y.
- **The Romanian/personal background story.** Mick doesn't need it. Lead with TiU credentials (BAOR alumni, Cristian backing).
- **Anything aspirational for "Phase 2" features that don't exist yet.** If a feature isn't built, don't promise it. Say *"that's on the roadmap; the pilot is about validating the core."*

If a question pulls toward any of these, the deflection pattern is: *"That's a fair question — I'd rather come back to it after we've talked through whether the core fits TiU. The answer changes depending on what we learn from the pilot."*

## Mick's likely incentives — what he wants for himself

External Affairs Officers at Dutch universities are typically measured (formally or informally) on a handful of things:

1. **Number of corporate partnerships maintained or grown.** More logos on the careers page, more recurring relationships.
2. **Quality of those partnerships.** Premium employers, branded events, big career fair sponsors.
3. **Visible engagement metrics.** Postings, applications, event attendance — anything he can report up the chain.
4. **Operational efficiency.** Less manual back-office work, fewer support fires, less complaining from career services staff.
5. **Personal credit for innovation.** Bringing in a new tool, partnership, or program that gets named in an annual report.

Notice what's *not* on this list: how happy individual students are, how good the matching algorithm is, whether SMEs can afford to post. Those things matter to *you*; they don't move *him* directly. **Reframe everything you say in terms of these five incentives.**

## The strategic posture for the meeting

You're not pitching a product. You're enrolling a co-conspirator.

The frame: **"I want to build something that solves problems you have. Help me understand what those are."**

If that frame holds for the first 15 minutes, the rest of the meeting writes itself. If you skip the listening and go straight to the pitch, you'll trigger his defenses.

Three principles:

1. **Make Mick the hero of the story.** The conversation should leave him feeling like he discovered Rookies, not like he was sold to. He should feel like Rookies is something he could bring to Herbert as *his* find, *his* initiative, *his* contribution. Frame everything that way.
2. **Position Rookies as something built FOR him, not as something he should adopt.** Listen to his pain points first. Then say "interesting, that's exactly the kind of problem we'd want to solve. Tell me more about how that plays out for you week-to-week." Then later: "Given what you said earlier, here's how Rookies would handle that."
3. **Defer to his expertise constantly.** "You'd know better than me — does this seem realistic?" / "I'd want to learn from how you'd want to use this." / "What would Career Services pushback be?" His expertise is real; using it is genuine respect, not flattery.

## The meeting: minute-by-minute structure

**Plan for 45–60 minutes. Adjust on the fly.**

### Minutes 0–3 — opening + frame-setting

Walk in calm, confident, but not aggressive. Open with:

> *"Mick, thanks for taking the time. Cristian shared your thoughts on the deck and I really appreciated how detailed they were — they sharpened my thinking before this meeting more than anything else has. Before I show you anything, I'd actually rather understand your situation first. You've been running TiU's external-affairs function for years; you've worked with JobTeaser long enough to know exactly where it works and where it doesn't. I'd love to hear that perspective before I tell you anything about Rookies. Some of what I've built may be relevant; some may not. I'd rather get your input first than waste your time pitching something that doesn't fit."*

Then **stop talking.** Genuinely listen.

### Minutes 3–18 — listening phase

Ask open-ended questions and let him talk. **Take notes.** Visible note-taking signals that you take his words seriously.

Questions to land in this order:

1. *"What does a great month look like for you in this role? What are you trying to drive for TiU externally?"*
2. *"When you think about JobTeaser specifically — what's working? What does it solve for you operationally?"*
3. *"What doesn't it solve? What still takes too much of your time, or doesn't go the way you'd want?"*
4. *"You mentioned 1-on-1 personal guidance is the core of what Career Services does. Where do you see the platform fitting in — is it support for that work, or is it parallel?"*
5. *"What kind of metrics do you report up to Herbert and TiU's management? What does success look like to them?"*
6. *"If a vendor came to you tomorrow with something genuinely new — what would they need to show you for you to take it seriously to Herbert?"*
7. *"Are there any types of employers you wish you could attract more of? Any gaps in TiU's current corporate partnership mix?"*

The seventh question is the trapdoor. If he says *"yes, we struggle with mid-size and SME employers — they don't engage with the portal,"* you have a perfect bridge to your SME-accessibility advantage. If he doesn't volunteer that, you'll need to introduce it more carefully later.

**Listen for these keywords.** When he uses them, you know what to lean into:

- *"Reputation"*, *"profile"*, *"visibility"* → External Affairs is judged on TiU's market position
- *"Partnerships"*, *"corporate relations"*, *"key accounts"* → his bread and butter
- *"Reporting"*, *"metrics"*, *"AD"*, *"Herbert"* → he'll need a justification framework
- *"Time"*, *"manual"*, *"back office"*, *"team capacity"* → operational pain
- *"International"*, *"diversity"*, *"underrepresented"* → DEI mandate
- *"Curriculum"*, *"skills"*, *"employability"* → strategic pressure from faculty

### Minutes 18–35 — bridge to Rookies

Now you transition. Use a phrase like:

> *"Several things you said line up with problems we've been trying to solve. Let me show you a few specific things and you tell me whether they'd help — or where they wouldn't."*

Then walk through Rookies, but reframe each feature as a response to something he said. **Cite him back.** This is the entire game.

For example, if he mentioned operational time:
> *"You said the back office is what makes JobTeaser work — that the support is what saves your team time. Honest answer: at pilot scale, that back office is me. Personally available, as-soon-as-possible response. If we move past pilot and it's working for both sides, we hire the team that takes support from founder-led to vendor-level. I'm not pretending we have that capacity now."*

If he mentioned reporting up to Herbert:
> *"You said you report engagement metrics up to the AD. Here's something that might be additional ammunition for those reports — a real-time skills-gap dashboard showing what employers searched for at TiU vs what your students have. Curriculum-relevant data the existing platform doesn't deliver. Different audience for different data."*

If he mentioned struggling with SME engagement:
> *"That's exactly the bottleneck we've been trying to solve. JobTeaser's pricing locks small employers out by design — free is capped at 4 postings, premium is gated to firms hiring 5+ per year. So TiU ends up with the same well-funded names recruiting on the platform. On Rookies, small employers post without artificial caps — that's the structural fix."*

If he mentioned recruiter pain or noisy candidate search:
> *"On Rookies, recruiters don't browse a talent bank that students opted into — they see every active TiU student ranked against their listing, with the breakdown of why. One click invites a candidate to apply, with pre-filled message templates. The whole outreach loop happens in the platform, not in five separate tools."*

If he mentioned employment-outcome reporting or accreditation pressure:
> *"One thing we do that no other platform in this space does — we keep tracking students past the application. Did they get placed? Where? In what role? Even if it happened off-platform, we capture it. Over time, that gives TiU's management longitudinal placement-outcome data — which skills correlated with which placements, how the industry's demand shifted year-over-year. It's the data your accreditation reviews and curriculum committees actually need, and it builds a research-grade dataset for TiU specifically."*

If he mentioned 1-on-1 guidance philosophy:
> *"You're right — students don't get jobs because of an app. They get jobs because someone helped them think through their direction. Rookies isn't trying to replace what your team does. It's trying to make those conversations more grounded — by giving you and your team data on what students are actually engaging with, where they're stuck, and what employers are searching for that nobody on staff knows about. The platform makes your work sharper, not redundant."*

**Bring out the live demo only when he's curious, not as a default.** Don't force the demo if the conversation is moving well in pure verbal mode. If you do demo, it's the matching engine + the skills-gap dashboard, in that order. Five minutes max.

### Minutes 35–45 — make him the hero

This is where you flip the dynamic. Stop offering things; start asking him to shape it.

> *"Honestly, the version of this that would actually work at TiU is the version you'd help shape. If you were going to imagine taking Rookies to Herbert in three weeks, what would the framing need to be? What would TiU's management want to see? What would the obvious objections be from your colleagues?"*

What this does:

- Activates his expertise (real, valued)
- Gets him mentally rehearsing pitching it to Herbert (which is what you want)
- Makes him part of the design, not the buyer of someone else's design
- Surfaces objections proactively so you can address them now

If he engages with this question, you've won more than half the room. He's now mentally in "how do I bring this in?" mode rather than "should I let this in?" mode.

### Minutes 45–55 — concrete next-step proposal

Don't end vaguely. Propose something specific and small:

> *"Here's what I'd suggest as a low-commitment next step: I send you the response memo addressing the points you raised in your email — short, written, so you have something on paper. Cristian and I would be happy to come back in 2-3 weeks for a follow-up where, depending on what you think, we either talk about what a parallel pilot at TiSEM might look like, or you tell us what's still missing. No pressure on you to commit to anything today; the question is just whether you'd find it useful to keep exploring."*

The win here is he agrees to *exploring further*, not to *adopting Rookies*. That's the right ask for a screening meeting.

### Minutes 55–60 — close warmly

Thank him sincerely. Ask if there's anything you can send him afterwards. Don't oversell. Walk out before he gets impatient.

## Anticipated objections — specific responses

**"I'm a fan of JobTeaser. It works for what we need."**

> *"You should be — by the criteria you've described, it does what it needs to. We're not trying to replace something that works. We're trying to fill three gaps that are structurally outside what JobTeaser delivers — matching at the core, skills-gap intelligence for TiU's management, and SME accessibility. Run us in parallel during the pilot, and the question after September is whether those three things are worth a partnership. Not whether you should switch off what you have."*

**"How do you acquire companies?"**

This was raised in his email — expect it directly. The trick: make *founder-led, hand-to-hand* sound like the strategy it is, not the absence of one. Specifics beat hand-waving.

> *"Honest answer — for the pilot, this is hand-to-hand. Target: 20–30 named employer partners committed in writing by launch day. Three channels I'd actually use:*

> *One — my network and Cristian's. Brabant boutiques, Tilburg tech, alumni-led firms. Highest warmth, easiest yes.*

> *Two — Tilburg-region channels. BOM (Brabantse Ontwikkelings Maatschappij), Brabantse Werkgevers Vereniging, MKB Brabant. Targeted outreach framed as 'TiU is partnering with Rookies; would your firm like founding-employer-partner access?'*

> *Three — TiU-adjacent companies that already recruit here through Career Week or alumni events. You'd know more of those firms than I do. If a pilot moves forward, even ten warm intros from you to companies you'd vouch for would be a meaningful accelerant — only if you saw that as appropriate.*

> *Past the pilot, the channel mix changes — references, sector-specific outreach, eventually some inbound. But for the next 4–6 months, it's hand-to-hand. I'm not pretending we have a sales engine we don't."*

**Why this works:** specifics beat hand-waving. Naming BOM and Brabantse Werkgevers Vereniging signals you've thought about this regionally. Inviting Mick into the channel mix activates him as a co-conspirator — even if he doesn't immediately commit, the question puts him in a posture of mentally listing companies he could intro you to. That's enrollment in action.

**Don't say:** *"We'll run paid acquisition / LinkedIn campaigns / content marketing."* Those phrases signal you're imagining a sales motion you don't have. Hand-to-hand, named, regional, founder-led is the right answer at your stage. Own it.

**"What about back-office support? JobTeaser has a real team."**

> *"You're right. At pilot scale, the back office is me — direct phone, fast turnaround, no ticket queue. That's actually a benefit at this stage; you're not waiting on tier-2 support. Past pilot, the funding from a longer-term partnership pays for the team that scales like JobTeaser's does. I'm not going to pretend we have that capacity today. But I'm also fully responsive in a way they can't be."*

**"Have you considered TiU's processes are aligned with JobTeaser?"**

> *"I have, and that's actually why I'm not asking you to switch. I'm asking for a pilot in parallel — JobTeaser stays in place, your existing workflows don't change, your team doesn't have to relearn anything. Rookies covers two specific gaps — SME visibility and skills-gap intelligence — that JobTeaser doesn't address. After September, we have data to compare and you decide whether it's worth continuing."*

**"You're looking for an investor through TiU?"**

(This is the misperception from his email — correct it gently and immediately.)

> *"To be very clear — we're not looking for funding from TiU. The ask is partnership: a free pilot through September, then a conversation about whether a longer-term arrangement makes sense based on what the pilot shows. No money exchanged during the pilot."*

> Important: do NOT volunteer details about how Rookies plans to monetize companies down the road. If Mick presses on long-term commercial terms, deflect: *"Honestly, that's a conversation for after September. The pilot's job is to show whether this delivers value for TiU. If it does, the model gets co-designed with TiU's management. If it doesn't, there's nothing to monetize."* Keeps the conversation about value to TiU, not about your business model.

**"What's in this for me, operationally?"**

(This may not be asked directly, but it's the question under everything.)

> *"Four things, honestly. One — the skills-gap dashboard gives you data you can take to Herbert and TiU's management that the existing platform doesn't deliver. Two — longitudinal placement-outcome tracking. Most platforms stop after the application; we keep going. You get year-over-year data on which TiU students placed where, in what roles, with what skills — exactly the data accreditation reviews and curriculum committees ask for. Three — bringing SMEs into the recruitment pool diversifies your corporate-partnership mix without you having to chase every boutique firm yourself. Four — being the person who brought this in. If Rookies works at TiU, you're the External Affairs Officer who set up the founding partnership. That's a story that travels."*

**"What if students don't engage?"**

> *"Real risk. Two answers. One — that's exactly the kind of question a 4-month pilot is designed to answer. We agree on what engagement looks like up front, measure it, and decide based on data, not theory. Two — student engagement is partly platform UX (our problem) and partly the meta-experience of caring about career-search (your team's offline work). Rookies works best when it's an extension of your team's existing relationship-building, not a substitute. Which is why we want to design this with you, not for you."*

## Three things to internalize before walking in

1. **Mick has more power than you do in this conversation.** Respect that. Acknowledge it implicitly through how you speak. Founders who walk into university meetings acting like equals lose; founders who walk in as students-of-the-system win.

2. **A "no" today doesn't mean a "no" forever.** Even if he kills it for the management meeting, you've learned what a Dutch career-services manager objects to. That information is gold for the next pitch — Erasmus, Maastricht, whoever. Treat the meeting as research even if it's a sales call.

3. **The single biggest predictor of how this goes is whether Mick feels heard.** Not whether he's convinced, not whether the matching algorithm is impressive, not whether the dashboard mockup is sharp. **Whether he feels heard.** Listen first. Reflect his concerns back. Cite him in your responses. The product talk is downstream of that.

Walk in calm. Listen hard. Speak less than you think you should. Get a low-commitment yes. That's the whole game.
