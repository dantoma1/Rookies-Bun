# Rookies — Question & Objection Playbook

*For: Dan Toma Poenaru · May 7 meeting with Mick Timmermans*
*Use: read once carefully, mark the responses you find sharpest, internalize the cadence — do NOT memorize verbatim. Verbatim recitations sound rehearsed.*

---

## How to use this document

Each section groups questions Mick (or anyone in a similar position) is likely to raise. Each entry has:

- **The question** — in Mick's likely phrasing
- **The response** — what to say (or close to it)
- **The follow-up if pushed** — when the first answer doesn't land
- **Watch for** — tone, body language, what to avoid

The responses are designed to be:
- Honest. No claims you can't back.
- In his vocabulary, not startup-speak.
- Acknowledgment-first. Every response begins by validating something in his concern.
- Bridging. Each one connects back to the value proposition without being heavy-handed.

If you read one section in detail before walking in, make it Section IX (the deflection scripts on commercial topics). That is where most founders accidentally give away too much.

## The opening — what separates Rookies from JobTeaser

Before any Q&A, you should be able to deliver this in 90 seconds, calmly, without notes.

> *"Rookies and JobTeaser look superficially similar — both connect students with employers through a university-branded portal. The differences are structural, not surface-level.*

> *First, our matching is the core of the platform — built in, transparent, free to use. JobTeaser's matching is behind a paid tier, which is why TiU doesn't currently use it.*

> *Second, we deliver skills-gap intelligence to TiU's management — a real-time view of what employers are searching for vs. what TiU students have. Curriculum-relevant data the existing platform doesn't surface.*

> *Third, small employers can post freely on Rookies, without the artificial caps that lock SMEs out of JobTeaser. That's the structural fix for why your students see only the same well-funded firms.*

> *Fourth, companies reach the right ranked students directly — one click, no opt-in walls. Recruiters don't have to fall back to LinkedIn or noisy keyword search.*

> *Fifth, we track student outcomes longitudinally — what happens after placement, even off-platform. Year-over-year, it gives TiU data on which skills and which paths actually lead to good outcomes.*

> *Everything else — events, ATS integrations, orientation tools — JobTeaser does well today, and we're not trying to compete on that surface. We're filling specific gaps that are structurally outside what JobTeaser was designed to deliver."*

That paragraph is the spine. Every Q&A response below is, ultimately, a refraction of one of those five points back through whatever angle Mick approaches you from.

## I. Questions about differentiation

These are Mick's most likely opening probes. He has already written that he doesn't see a distinguishing factor; expect him to test that thesis directly.

---

### Q: "I'm a fan of JobTeaser. It works for what we need. Why should we add another platform?"

**Response:**
> *"You should be a fan — by the criteria you've described, JobTeaser does what it needs to. We're not asking you to switch off something that works. We're asking whether there's a complementary layer that adds something specifically to TiU's strategic position that JobTeaser wasn't designed to deliver. Run us in parallel for the pilot, and the question after September is whether those specific gaps are worth a partnership. Not whether you should replace what you have."*

**If pushed:**
> *"The cleanest framing might be — JobTeaser is your operational platform; we'd be your strategic data and SME-accessibility layer. They serve different jobs. They don't compete; they complement."*

**Watch for:** Don't argue against JobTeaser. Agree that it works. The win is moving the conversation from *replace* to *parallel*.

---

### Q: "I don't really see a distinguishing factor yet. What's actually different?"

**Response:**
> *"That's fair — most of what we've sent so far has been written, and written summaries flatten the differences. Three things that are structurally different and worth seeing visually: the matching engine, the skills-gap dashboard, and how recruiters experience student discovery. If you have ten minutes, I can walk through them on the live platform. Tell me which would land hardest for you and I'll start there."*

**If pushed:**
> *"The single sharpest thing — what JobTeaser charges behind a paid tier, we make as the core. Matching is the engine of our platform, not a module. That's hard to convey on paper but obvious in the demo."*

**Watch for:** This is the moment to pull out the laptop. Demo the matching first — it is your sharpest visual differentiator. Don't show all five advantages; show the one that lands hardest for the audience in the room.

---

### Q: "How is your matching different from JobTeaser's matching?"

**Response:**
> *"Three things. One — ours is built into the core for both sides. Every student sees jobs ranked with a transparent score and breakdown; every recruiter sees students ranked the same way. JobTeaser's matching, when it exists, is a paid module on the recruiter side only and the algorithm is opaque. Two — ours is tunable per institution. If TiU thinks skills should weigh more heavily than location, that's a one-line adjustment. JobTeaser's is a vendor black box. Three — ours is bidirectional and consistent. The 73% match a student sees against a job is the same 73% the company sees for that student. Symmetric trust."*

**Watch for:** Avoid technical jargon. He wants to know what it does, not how it does it.

---

### Q: "Universities everywhere talk about matchmaking. Why do you think yours is different?"

**Response:**
> *"You're right — every vendor pitches matching. Honest answer: most matching products don't actually surface meaningful differentiation between candidates because they keyword-match. Ours uses ten dimensions including skills, sector, location, school year, role interests, and field of study, with weighted scoring and transparent breakdowns per match. But the proof is the demo, not the description. Let me show you a candidate's actual ranked list and then judge whether it looks like meaningful matching or like keyword search."*

**Watch for:** Cede the point that the category is crowded with vendors saying the same thing. Then redirect to the demo as the only honest evaluator.

## II. Questions about the company and the team

These come from a place of risk assessment. Mick wants to know whether you're real, whether you'll still be here in 12 months, whether his bet on you is safe.

---

### Q: "Tell me about your team. Who's behind this?"

**Response:**
> *"Cristian and I have been working on this together for the last several months — Cristian providing academic guidance and acting as a conceptual sounding board, me running product and engineering. The technical execution has been principally me, with Tudor (my brother) as a founding engineer focused on the back-end. We're a small team, deliberately. Past pilot, if it's working, we'd add senior engineering and operational staff as the platform earns the right to have them."*

**If pushed:**
> *"I'm conscious that small teams carry risk. The flip side is that during the pilot, you have direct, unmediated access to the founder. You're not waiting on a tier-2 support queue."*

**Watch for:** Don't overstate the team. Don't undersell either. Mick has been pitched by overclaimers; what gets respect is calibrated honesty.

---

### Q: "Are you doing this full-time?"

**Response:**
> *"Effectively yes — the work has been my full focus alongside finishing the master's, and post-graduation it's the primary thing. I'm not splitting attention."*

**If pushed about the brother / second founder:**
> *"My brother Tudor is a BSc Computer Science and Engineering student at TU/e. He works on Rookies alongside school. He's not titled co-founder; he's a founding engineer. I'm clear-eyed about what that does and doesn't add to our credibility."*

**Watch for:** Honest framing here matters. Don't claim full-time team if the reality is otherwise. Mick will figure it out, and what he respects is the person who told him before he found out.

---

### Q: "What if you go out of business? What happens to our data and our students?"

**Response:**
> *"Fair concern, and one we've thought about. Three things. One — all data is hosted on Supabase in the EU, which gives you continuity guarantees independent of us. Two — at the start of the pilot we'd put in writing exactly what happens if we wind down: full data export to TiU, a 90-day grace period during which we'd assist migration. Three — the pilot is structured precisely to limit your exposure. You're not committing to anything beyond September. The downside is bounded."*

**If pushed:**
> *"The realistic risk is not that we disappear; it's that we stay small and don't grow as fast as we hope. In which case the platform keeps working at TiU but we never become a regional player. That's a much more likely failure mode than a sudden shutdown."*

**Watch for:** Don't promise infrastructure you don't have (don't claim "automated daily backups" if you don't). Be specific about what you can credibly commit to.

---

### Q: "Have you raised funding? Are you planning to?"

**Response:**
> *"Not yet, and this isn't a pitch for TiU funding. The pilot is bootstrapped by the team. Past September, we'd raise institutional capital based on the pilot results, but that's downstream of this conversation, not the reason for it."*

**If pushed:**
> *"Honestly, the order matters. Investors want to see a working partnership and traction. So the goal of the pilot is to validate the partnership; once that's clear, we raise. Our being unfunded today is part of why the pilot has to land — but it also means we're not yet entangled with investor priorities that might conflict with TiU's."*

**Watch for:** Do NOT volunteer projected raise size, target investors, or commercial assumptions. Stay vague but honest about the sequencing.

## III. Questions about the product

These tend to be more relaxed — Mick is in evaluator mode, not gatekeeper mode. Take advantage of the lighter tone.

---

### Q: "How do students sign up?"

**Response:**
> *"Email and password today, with a confirmation link. Light onboarding flow that captures profile data — education, skills, preferences. About 10 minutes if a student fills it in fully. Past pilot, we'd integrate with TiU's identity system so students can sign in with their TiU credentials — that's the kind of integration that meaningfully reduces friction at scale."*

**Follow-up if asked about SSO:**
> *"We support generic SSO standards. Specific to TiU, the integration would go through SURFconext — that's a 1–2 week piece of work for IT once we're past pilot. Happy to scope this with whoever runs IT integration on your end."*

**Watch for:** Don't promise SSO is live today if it isn't. Frame it as a roadmap item, doable, but post-pilot.

---

### Q: "What data do you collect on students?"

**Response:**
> *"Profile data — name, university, degree, skills, location preferences, field of study, language proficiency, work authorization. Application activity — which roles they applied to, status. Messages they exchange with employers. Plus the optional CV and avatar uploads. We don't collect anything beyond what students explicitly enter or upload. They can export everything they've shared, or delete it entirely, from a single Settings page. GDPR-compliant by design."*

**Follow-up if asked specifically about GDPR:**
> *"Three things. One — data is hosted in the EU. Two — students have one-click data export and account deletion. Three — we publish a Privacy Policy that names us as data controller and lists every third-party processor we use. We have a draft ready to share with TiU's privacy office."*

**Watch for:** GDPR is not abstract for him. TiU's privacy office will eventually review this. Sound like you've thought about it. Don't oversell readiness — say "draft ready for review," not "fully audited and ironclad."

---

### Q: "Is there a mobile app?"

**Response:**
> *"Mobile-responsive web today, no native app. The product is built mobile-first — most student usage in our testing has been on phones — but it's a web experience, not an iOS or Android binary. A native app would be an obvious post-pilot investment if engagement metrics support it."*

**Watch for:** Don't promise a mobile app that doesn't exist. Frame web-mobile as a deliberate choice today and native as a future capability.

---

### Q: "How do you verify employers? What stops a fake company from posting?"

**Response:**
> *"Each employer goes through a verification step before they can post — currently a manual review by us. We check the company website, the work email domain, public registration data. For pilot scale this is manageable manually. Past pilot, we'd add automation. We've seen this matter — fake-job spam is a real problem on this kind of platform — and it's something we've designed against from the start."*

**Watch for:** This is Mick's territory (employer relationships). Show that you respect the seriousness of it. Don't promise zero spam — that's not credible. Promise active management.

---

### Q: "How do you handle CV uploads — what about plagiarized or AI-generated CVs?"

**Response:**
> *"Honest answer — we don't currently scan CVs for plagiarism or AI-generation. The platform stores them as documents and presents them to employers; the verification of authenticity sits on the employer side. We could add LLM-based screening as a feature later, but the realistic risk profile is that employers do their own check, which they currently do anyway. The bigger CV question for us is making sure students don't upload something that exposes private data they didn't intend to share."*

**Watch for:** This is one of those questions where being honest about what you don't do is more credible than fabricating a feature.

---

### Q: "Can students hide their profile from specific companies — like their current employer?"

**Response:**
> *"Yes. Profile visibility has three settings — Community, Employers only, and Private. We can also add a per-company block list as a feature; it's on the roadmap but not built yet. The general visibility controls work today and address the most common case, which is a student wanting to be discoverable to employers without their profile showing in the public student community."*

**Watch for:** Be honest about what's built vs roadmap. The block-specific-company case is a real one, common enough to mention as a planned addition.

## IV. Questions about operations and support

This is Mick's emotional center. He has been burned by vendors who promised support and delivered tickets-into-a-void. He needs to know how operational reality looks.

---

### Q: "How do you acquire companies? Where will the employer side come from?"

**This was raised in his email — expect it directly.** It is one of the most common "weak point" moments in early-stage B2B pitches. The trick is to make *founder-led, hand-to-hand* sound like a strategy (which it is at your stage) rather than an absence of strategy. Honest specificity beats glossy hand-waving.

**Response:**
> *"Honest answer — for the pilot, this is hand-to-hand. The target is 20–30 named employer partners committed in writing by launch day, and that's me on the phone, not a sales engine. Three channels I'd actually use:*

> *One — my network and Cristian's. Brabant boutiques, Tilburg tech, alumni-led firms. Highest warmth, easiest yes.*

> *Two — Tilburg-region channels. BOM (Brabantse Ontwikkelings Maatschappij) maintains a database of regional SMEs they fund or partner with. Brabantse Werkgevers Vereniging and MKB Brabant are similar pools. Targeted outreach, framed as 'TiU is partnering with Rookies — would your firm like founding-employer-partner access?'*

> *Three — TiU-adjacent companies that already recruit here through Career Week or alumni events. They know TiU; they just need a different access point. And honestly — you'd know more of those firms than I do. If a pilot moves forward, even ten warm intros from you to companies you'd vouch for would be a meaningful accelerant. Only if you saw that as appropriate, of course — but I'd rather flag the value of your network than pretend I'm doing this without help.*

> *Past the pilot, the channel mix changes — references from successful early companies, sector-specific outreach, eventually some inbound. But for the next 4–6 months, it's hand-to-hand. I'm not pretending we have a sales engine we don't."*

**If pushed on what verticals:**
> *"Four sectors with natural fit and TiU student demand: boutique consulting and advisory, regional tech and data, sustainability and circular economy, and finance / fintech-adjacent. Going broad later; going deep first."*

**If pushed on numbers / volume:**
> *"For the pilot, 20–30 named partners is the realistic target. Could be more if your network opens doors faster. The number isn't the metric — engagement and posting volume per partner is. A pilot with 25 active employers posting regularly outperforms a pilot with 100 logos and no activity."*

**Watch for:** This is the moment to invite Mick into the channel mix. Asking for his network is also a soft-yes-muscle exercise. He starts mentally listing companies he could intro you to — and that's enrollment in action, regardless of whether he commits. The phrasing *"only if you saw that as appropriate, of course"* is the guardrail that keeps it from feeling like a request for a favor.

**Don't say:** *"We'll do paid acquisition / LinkedIn campaigns / content marketing"* — those answers signal that you're imagining a sales motion you don't have. They make Mick less confident, not more. Hand-to-hand, named, regional, founder-led is the right answer at your stage. Own it.

---

### Q: "What's your back-office support set-up? When something breaks at 4pm on a Tuesday, who fixes it?"

**Response:**
> *"For the pilot, that's me. Direct phone, as-soon-as-possible support on critical issues, immediate Slack-style messaging if you have one. Past pilot, if it's working for both sides, we hire to take support from founder-led to vendor-level. I'm not pretending we have that capacity now. But I'm fully responsive in a way they can't be — you're not waiting on tier-2 escalation."*

**If pushed:**
> *"Realistic expectation: in the pilot, response time will be faster than JobTeaser's during business hours, slower outside them, because there's no overnight team. We'd document this clearly so there are no surprises about what to expect."*

**Watch for:** Don't promise 24/7 support. Don't pretend you have a team. Owning the constraint candidly is more credible than papering over it.

---

### Q: "Who supports our staff? Will Career Services need training?"

**Response:**
> *"Yes, briefly. Maybe an hour of orientation for staff who'll be administrating the platform — uploading the school logo, configuring the dashboard for TiU's management, validating company sign-ups if you want to keep that workflow. We'd run that session live, plus written documentation. The administrator interface is intentionally simple; this isn't a Salesforce-grade rollout."*

**Watch for:** "Hour of training" is honest and reassuring; "no training needed" is implausible and triggers his skepticism.

---

### Q: "What about exam periods — what happens when traffic spikes?"

**Response:**
> *"Honestly, our infrastructure scales linearly with usage at this volume — we're built on Supabase, which handles a few thousand concurrent users without intervention. We've not load-tested at TiU-wide scale because we haven't needed to. If the pilot succeeds and we cross volume thresholds, infrastructure scaling is a routine operational task, not a redesign. I'm flagging this as a place where I'm honest about what we haven't yet stress-tested."*

**Watch for:** This question reveals Mick is thinking operationally. The answer should be technical-but-honest: yes we can handle it, no we haven't yet proven it, here's what would happen if traffic spiked.

---

### Q: "How do you handle complaints from students?"

**Response:**
> *"Three layers. One — direct email to support, monitored by me during the pilot. Two — a feedback widget inside the app, so students can report problems contextually. Three — we'd want a regular check-in with your Career Services team where they surface things they're hearing from students that we wouldn't otherwise see. The third one matters more than the first two, because student-side complaints about platform UX often surface to staff before they surface to vendors."*

**Watch for:** This is a chance to acknowledge that Mick's team is the first to hear complaints. Treat them as your eyes and ears.

## V. Questions about the pilot

These are the ones where, if you have answers ready, the conversation moves from "should we explore?" to "how would we structure?" That shift is what you're aiming for.

---

### Q: "What does success look like for the pilot?"

**Response:**
> *"That's exactly the conversation I want to have, and I'd want it to be your numbers, not mine. As a starting point, success metrics that would be meaningful:*

> *• Number of TiU students with active, completed Rookies profiles by August*
> *• Number of employer postings live during the pilot*
> *• Match-score-driven applications submitted*
> *• Skills-gap dashboard delivered to TiU's management monthly*
> *• Student NPS from a single end-of-pilot survey*

> *The numbers themselves I'd want to set with you — what would make you feel confident standing in front of Herbert in September and saying it worked? That's the right anchor."*

**Watch for:** Resist quoting numbers as targets. Don't say "1,000 active profiles" — say "let's set the number together." Co-design is the move.

---

### Q: "What if it doesn't work?"

**Response:**
> *"Then we don't continue past September, and we both walk away with useful information. We'd document what we learned and TiU keeps its data. JobTeaser stays in place throughout the pilot, so 'doesn't work' doesn't leave you exposed — it just means the parallel layer didn't add the value we hoped it would. The pilot is structured so the downside is bounded for you."*

**Watch for:** "Bounded downside" is the key phrase. Mick's risk-assessment is heavily weighted; this language calms it.

---

### Q: "Who runs the pilot day-to-day at TiU?"

**Response:**
> *"On our side, me — I'd be the operational point person. On TiU's side, that depends on how you want to structure it. The smoothest version has a single named contact in your office who's the official liaison; doesn't have to be a heavy time commitment, maybe a 30-minute weekly check-in. We'd want to keep Cristian involved at TiSEM as the faculty backing, but the operational rhythm should sit with whoever in Career Services makes sense."*

**Watch for:** Implicitly invites Mick to be that contact. Don't push him into it; let him volunteer or designate someone. Either is fine.

---

### Q: "How do students find out about Rookies during the pilot?"

**Response:**
> *"That's the partnership piece. Three channels we'd want access to, if you're open to it:*

> *• Orientation week / introductory communications to incoming and final-year cohorts*
> *• Career services newsletter or email blast — one mention at launch, follow-up at midpoint*
> *• Student portal placement, ideally a card or banner from the existing TiU career page*

> *The TiSEM connection through Cristian gives us a faculty-level entry point too. I want to be careful here — we don't want to replace the channels you already use to promote JobTeaser; we want a parallel introduction that frames Rookies as an addition, not a replacement."*

**Watch for:** Treat his communication channels as his to control. Don't propose to take them over. Propose access, with him as the gatekeeper.

---

### Q: "How long is the pilot? What's the timeline?"

**Response:**
> *"From signed agreement to launch — about 3-4 weeks, mostly time for IT/privacy review on your side and for us to load TiU branding into the platform. From launch through September is the active pilot. Then a retrospective in late September, ideally with TiU's management, where we look at the metrics and decide whether to continue. So roughly 4 months of active operation, plus 1 month of setup before and 1 month of evaluation after."*

**Watch for:** The exact dates depend on when the management meeting happens and when an MoU gets signed. Stay flexible.

## VI. Questions about JobTeaser specifically

These are politically charged. Whatever you say will be repeated, possibly to JobTeaser's account manager. Be careful.

---

### Q: "Will running Rookies in parallel cause problems with our JobTeaser contract?"

**Response:**
> *"Worth checking — but I'd be surprised. JobTeaser doesn't typically have exclusivity clauses with universities at the contract level; their leverage is the network effect, not legal restriction. Your contract terms would be the source of truth. If there's an exclusivity provision, we'd need to know that before we launch. I'd assume not unless your legal team flags otherwise."*

**Follow-up if asked about JobTeaser potentially pulling out:**
> *"Unlikely they'd treat a parallel pilot as breach. Universities run pilots with multiple vendors regularly. If they did push back, that itself would be informative about the relationship, but I don't expect it."*

**Watch for:** Don't speak ill of JobTeaser. Stay neutral and professional. He may be testing whether you'll be diplomatic with their account manager later.

---

### Q: "What happens to TiSEM's existing platform during this?"

**Response:**
> *"That's actually one of the reasons I think a TiSEM pilot has the lowest friction — TiSEM is already operating outside the central JobTeaser deployment, so adding Rookies doesn't disrupt a workflow that doesn't exist there. Whether Rookies replaces the current TiSEM platform, runs in parallel, or replaces nothing during the pilot is up to TiSEM. We'd be respectful of whatever Cristian and the TiSEM administration decide."*

**Watch for:** This is a politically loaded area. Cristian's role and the TiSEM angle are sensitive. Don't volunteer strong views about TiSEM's existing platform; defer to the people who own it.

---

### Q: "Have you talked to JobTeaser about this?"

**Response:**
> *"No. We're not pursuing a vendor-to-vendor partnership with them. The relationship that matters to me is between Rookies and TiU. JobTeaser is a competitor in the broad sense, but we're not trying to integrate with them or attack them publicly. We're focused on delivering value to TiU directly."*

**Watch for:** Some operational managers test whether you've been respectful of incumbent vendors. Brief, neutral, no editorializing.

---

### Q: "What if JobTeaser builds the same things you have?"

**Response:**
> *"They might, eventually. Their commercial structure makes it harder than it sounds — their matching is a paid module precisely because it's expensive to operate, and changing that means changing their revenue model. But you're right that we don't have a permanent moat at the feature level. Our defensibility comes from being faster to iterate, more tunable per institution, and from the relationships we build with the universities we partner with. If we're going to win this category, it's by being closer to our partners' needs than a 800-school network can be by definition."*

**Watch for:** Don't claim a permanent technical moat. Honest answers about competitive dynamics earn more respect than overconfident ones.

## VII. Questions about strategic fit

These come from TiU's management mentality — even when Mick is asking them, he is asking them on Herbert's behalf.

---

### Q: "Why TiU specifically? Are you pitching other universities?"

**Response:**
> *"TiU first, deliberately. Three reasons. One — Cristian's relationship and my BAOR background give us specific credibility here that's harder to replicate elsewhere. Two — TiU's profile, especially TiSEM's, is in fields where our matching algorithm performs best (econometrics, business analytics, marketing, finance). Three — Tilburg's geography matters; the Brabant SME network we'd onboard for the pilot is regional, not national. We're talking to other universities at an early stage, but TiU is the founding partner we're building toward — not one of many."*

**Watch for:** "Founding partner" is the magic phrase. It elevates TiU's role and signals seriousness about the relationship.

---

### Q: "What about the dean? Has Herbert been briefed?"

**Response:**
> *"Cristian has been the connection so far. Herbert was the recipient of the original partnership request, and the management-level conversation is exactly what this pilot is meant to lead toward. The today-and-now conversation is operational fit with you; the next layer is the strategic conversation with Herbert. I see this meeting as the screening step before that one, which is why I want your input on what the management-level pitch should look like."*

**Watch for:** This positions Mick as the upstream-of-Herbert decision-maker, which is what he wants to feel.

---

### Q: "What's your relationship with Cristian, exactly?"

**Response:**
> *"He was my professor for several courses during the master's, and he's my MSc thesis advisor. The Rookies project is separate from my thesis — different topic — but it grew out of conversations we had about the placement-and-matching problem in graduate-employment markets. He's been advisor and sounding board throughout. The decision to bring Rookies to TiU was joint."*

**Watch for:** Make Cristian's role clear without overstating it. He's a champion, not a co-founder.

---

### Q: "What if students don't engage during the pilot?"

**Response:**
> *"Real risk. Two answers. One — engagement isn't a single number; we'd want to track it across student segments. International students, master's vs. bachelor's, different schools. We may see strong engagement in some segments and weak in others — that's data, not failure. Two — engagement at month 1 is going to be different from engagement at month 3. Career platforms have a S-curve; the relevant question is the slope, not the launch numbers. We'd want to look at trend, not the early snapshot."*

**Watch for:** Sophistication about engagement metrics earns respect. Don't claim guaranteed engagement.

## VIII. Questions about culture and risk — the harder ones

These are the questions where Mick is testing whether you understand the operational and political reality of his world.

---

### Q: "I've seen many vendors come through here. Why are you different?"

**Response:**
> *"Most aren't. Honest answer — most vendors who walk through this office have a generic product, a generic pitch, and limited interest in customizing for TiU specifically. What's different about us, structurally:*

> *One, we're built around your specific student population — Dutch academic categories, BAOR-style fields, regional employer mix. Not retrofitted. Two, we're small enough that the founder is in the room rather than the salesperson. Three, we have an inside connection through Cristian who's already vouched for us. Four, the value proposition isn't a product feature list; it's specific data that TiU's management will want regardless of which platform delivers it.*

> *That said, you don't have to take my word that we're different. The pilot is a low-cost way to test whether we actually are."*

**Watch for:** Admitting that most vendors aren't different is a credibility-builder. Then differentiate.

---

### Q: "Why should we trust you with student data when you're a small startup?"

**Response:**
> *"Same answer I'd give any client — trust is earned, not asked for. Three things you have that should make this lower-risk than the question implies. One — data is hosted on Supabase in the EU, governed by GDPR, with your students retaining full data export and deletion rights. Two — the pilot is bounded. If something goes wrong, you can withdraw and your students' data comes with you. Three — we're small enough that you have direct visibility into how the data is handled, who has access, and what's done with it. Larger vendors give you SOC 2 reports; we give you the founder's phone number. Different but defensible."*

**Watch for:** Transparency about size as a feature, not a liability.

---

### Q: "What's stopping students from getting spammed by bad recruiters?"

**Response:**
> *"Three protections. One — every employer is verified before they can message anyone. Two — students control their visibility settings and can mute or block any specific company. Three — we monitor outbound messaging for volume patterns; recruiters who send a hundred templated messages in a day get flagged. Realistic answer: spam control is a constant operational job, not a solved problem. We design against it actively rather than promising zero spam."*

**Watch for:** Don't promise zero spam. Promise active management.

---

### Q: "What about cultural fit between Dutch students and how the platform works?"

**Response:**
> *"Honest answer — we built this with Dutch students in mind from day one. The categories are Dutch academic categories. The job types are stage / working student / graduate. The location filters default to Brabant and Randstad cities. The work auth options reflect the EU/non-EU/orientation-year reality of NL. That said, cultural fit is the kind of thing we'd actively want feedback on during the pilot. Some of the choices we've made may feel off in subtle ways we wouldn't see, and we'd want to learn from your team's eye."*

**Watch for:** Acknowledge his expertise on Dutch student culture. He has it; you have it too but less of it.

---

### Q: "Have you actually used JobTeaser? Do you know what we deal with?"

**Response:**
> *"I've used the TiU career portal — your JobTeaser deployment — as a student. Logged in, set up a profile, browsed jobs, applied to a few. I haven't used it as an administrator, which is your view. That's actually one of the reasons I'd want to spend time with you in the pilot — your view of it operationally is what I most need to learn from."*

**Watch for:** Honesty about your perspective limit. Don't claim to know his job; cede expertise.

---

## IX. The deflections — questions you should NOT answer fully

There are a category of questions where the right move is not to answer directly. Deflect, redirect, or briefly acknowledge without elaboration. The principle: anything about Rookies' commercial model beyond "the pilot is free" should be deflected.

---

### Q: "What's your business model long-term? Will you charge us?"

**Response:**
> *"That's the conversation I want to have *after* the pilot, when there's data on whether we delivered value. The honest answer is: I don't know yet what model makes sense for TiU specifically. The pilot is free — full stop. If we get to renewal in September, the model would be co-designed with TiU's management, not handed down by us. I'd rather not anchor on numbers before we know what value's there to anchor against."*

**If pushed:**
> *"Some kind of subscription model is the likely structure for universities, but it depends entirely on what value the pilot demonstrates. Forcing a number now feels premature — and frankly, gives you a worse position to negotiate from in September than starting fresh would."*

**Watch for:** Reframe your refusal to give numbers as a benefit to him (better negotiating position). DO NOT volunteer the €5–10k figure even if asked specifically. It anchors him at the wrong number.

---

### Q: "Will you charge companies?"

**Response:**
> *"Long-term that's how this category typically works. For the pilot, posting is free for every employer. After September, the structure is something we'd think through alongside the renewal conversation — but the core principle, that small employers should have free access, is non-negotiable for us. It's the structural fix to the supply problem we talked about."*

**If pushed for specifics:**
> *"I'd rather not go into specific tiers today. What I can say is that whatever model emerges has to keep SMEs accessible, because that's the founding insight of the platform. Anything that recreates JobTeaser's structural exclusion of SMEs would defeat our own purpose."*

**Watch for:** Affirm the principle (SMEs free) without revealing the specifics (€500/mo for premium employers, etc). The principle is the value to TiU; the price points are internal.

---

### Q: "What's your fundraising plan?"

**Response:**
> *"Post-pilot. Successful pilot at TiU is the milestone we'd raise on. The exact amount and timing depends on outcomes from the pilot. Right now we're focused on TiU, not on the capital markets."*

**If pushed:**
> *"Fundraising isn't relevant to whether the pilot delivers value to TiU, which is what should drive your decision. Happy to talk about it after we have results, but not as a determinant of this conversation."*

**Watch for:** Block this topic firmly but politely. Investors and TiU should not be intermingled in the same conversation; it confuses both.

---

### Q: "What's your team going to look like in 12 months?"

**Response:**
> *"Bigger than today, and shaped by what the pilot demonstrates. I'd expect a senior engineer, an operations lead, and possibly a customer-success role focused on the universities we work with. Specifics depend on funding and pilot outcomes."*

**Watch for:** Don't give org-chart specifics or hire timelines. Stay vague but credible.

---

### Q: "How are you going to scale this beyond TiU?"

**Response:**
> *"Slowly. Two universities deep before we do three. The playbook we're building with TiU — what works in Career Services rollout, what employers respond to, what students engage with — is the foundation. We'd take six months at a successful TiU before approaching a second institution. Scale follows depth, not the other way around."*

**Watch for:** Acknowledges scale ambitions without making TiU sound like a stepping stone. Both true things.

---

## X. The diplomatic deflections — when Mick wants to end politely

Some objections aren't about the substance; they're about ending the conversation without committing. Here's how to engage with them.

---

### "Let me think about it."

**Response:**
> *"Of course. Can I propose a frame for what to think about? The question I'd want you to answer for yourself is whether the pilot, as we'd structure it, has bounded enough downside that exploration is worth it — not whether you're confident Rookies will deliver on every promise. Those are different bars. The first one is much lower."*

**Then:** *"Mind if I follow up by email next week with a written response to the questions from your earlier note? That gives you something on paper to revisit and also lets us schedule a follow-up if you want one."*

**Watch for:** "Let me think about it" usually means "I want to end this without saying no." Reframing the decision (low bar to explore) gives him a face-saving way to keep the conversation alive.

---

### "I need to discuss this with the team."

**Response:**
> *"Makes sense. Anything I can prepare that would help that conversation? A short written brief, a demo recording, a one-pager on the data privacy approach — happy to provide whatever your team would find useful."*

**Watch for:** Offering to do work for him reduces his friction in advancing the conversation internally.

---

### "We'd need to bring this through procurement."

**Response:**
> *"Of course. Since the pilot is free, the procurement track may be lighter than for a standard vendor — but I'm happy to follow whatever process makes sense. Could you point me toward who I should connect with? Or is the cleanest version of this for me to send a brief to you and you'd route it?"*

**Watch for:** Don't fight the process. Route through whoever he tells you to. Volunteer to do the work.

---

### "This isn't really my decision."

**Response:**
> *"Understood. The way I see this conversation: Cristian asked you to be the operational lens before this goes to Herbert, because if it doesn't work operationally for you, it won't work for TiU at any level. Your assessment doesn't have to be a decision — but I'd value your honest read on whether this is worth taking to the management level, even just as a conversation."*

**Watch for:** This validates his position (he doesn't decide alone) while clarifying what you're actually asking him for (a take, not a decision).

---

### "We have a contract with JobTeaser through XYZ."

**Response:**
> *"That's exactly why a parallel pilot makes sense rather than asking you to switch vendors mid-contract. Run us alongside, don't disturb anything contractually, and the question at the natural renewal moment with JobTeaser becomes 'do we want to maintain status quo or restructure?' That's a much later, calmer conversation than what we'd be deciding today."*

**Watch for:** Always reframe contract concerns as reasons to do a parallel pilot, not reasons to defer the conversation.

---

## XI. The final-five-minutes questions

These are questions Mick may save until the meeting is winding down. They're often the ones that signal whether you've actually moved him.

---

### Q: "What would you need from me to move this forward?"

**Response:**
> *"Honestly — willingness to take this to Herbert as something worth a 30-minute conversation at the management level. That's it. Not endorsement, not commitment, just openness to making the introduction. Cristian and I would do the work of preparing for the management-level pitch. From your side, the ask is just the next door."*

**Watch for:** When Mick asks this question, he is offering you a real opening. The smallest possible ask is the right ask. "Open the next door."

---

### Q: "What are next steps?"

**Response:**
> *"Three things, ideally. One — I send you a short written response to the points from your email, this week, so you have something on paper. Two — we agree on what a parallel pilot at TiSEM would look like, in writing, that we can both align on. Three — once those are in shape, you and Cristian propose a follow-up with Herbert. Three to five weeks total, no rush."*

**Watch for:** Have a specific sequence ready. Nothing kills momentum faster than "let's see" when he's asking for structure.

---

### Q: "Can I send this to a colleague to get their take?"

**Response:**
> *"Absolutely. Who would be useful to include? If it's IT or privacy, I have draft technical and data-privacy briefs I could share that would speed those conversations. If it's another Career Services colleague, the deck and one-pager are probably enough. Tell me what they'd find useful and I'll get it to you."*

**Watch for:** Make it easy for him to advocate internally. Don't gate access to materials.

---

### Q: "When do you need an answer?"

**Response:**
> *"Not on a hard deadline. The natural cadence is: written response from me this week, your internal conversation over the next week or two, follow-up meeting in early June. If that's roughly your time horizon, we're aligned. If you need more time, we adapt — I'd rather move at the right pace than rush you into a no."*

**Watch for:** Be patient. Forcing urgency is a tell that you're more anxious than he is.

---

## XII. A few last-resort responses

If you're caught off-guard or completely lose the thread, these phrases buy you time and credibility:

- *"Honest answer — I don't know yet. Let me come back to you on that."* — Massively credibility-positive when used sparingly. Founders who never say it lose trust over a 45-minute conversation.

- *"That's a good point. I want to think about it more carefully before I give you a half-answer."* — Buys you time without bullshitting.

- *"You'd know better than me — what's your read on that?"* — Cedes the question back to him, keeps the conversation collaborative.

- *"Let me make sure I understand the question correctly — you're asking about [paraphrase]."* — Buys 20 seconds while the room stays calm.

- *"I'm conscious that's outside my expertise. I'd want to bring [Cristian / our advisor / your IT team] into that conversation rather than answer it myself."* — Honest deferral, not a dodge.

These are tools, not crutches. Use sparingly and only when you actually need them. Overusing them sounds evasive.

---

## XIII. The minute-zero opening — once more, with feeling

You walk in. Cristian is already there. Mick stands or stays seated, depending on his style. After the handshakes and the small talk:

> *"Mick, thank you for the time. Cristian, thanks for setting this up — I know what it took on your end to make it happen.*

> *Before I show you anything, I'd actually rather understand your situation first. You've been running TiU's external-affairs function for years; you've worked with JobTeaser long enough to know exactly where it works and where it doesn't. I'd love to hear that perspective before I tell you anything about Rookies. Some of what I've built may be relevant; some may not. I'd rather get your input first than waste your time pitching something that doesn't fit."*

Then stop. Take out the notebook. Write down the date and his name. Wait for him to start.

That's the most important 30 seconds of the entire meeting. The rest of this 8,000-word document supports it. If you nail those 30 seconds, the rest of the meeting has a shape it can grow into. If you don't, no amount of preparation salvages it.

You've done the work. Walk in calm. Listen hard. Let him talk. Speak less than you think you should. Get a low-commitment yes. That's the whole game.
